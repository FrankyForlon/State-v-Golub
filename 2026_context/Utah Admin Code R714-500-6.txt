# Utah Admin Code R714-500-6

Utah Admin Code R714-500-6 outlines the requirements and procedures for the certification of breath alcohol concentration testing instruments used for law enforcement purposes in Utah. The rule specifies the following key points:

- Instruments must provide accurate and consistent analysis of breath specimens for determining breath alcohol concentration.
- The analysis must be based on infra-red energy absorption or a similarly effective method approved by the Department of Public Safety.
- The sample analyzed should be alveolar or end expiratory.
- Results must be measured in grams of alcohol per 210 liters of breath.
- During certification checks, the instrument must analyze a reference sample according to standardized procedures.
- The Department reviews and approves all instruments, maintains a list of accepted brands/models, and requires all approved instruments to be routinely certified by a technician‚Äînot exceeding 40 days between checks.
- Certification checks involve several tests, including power, temperature, internal calibration, and reference sample checks, to ensure accuracy and compliance with departmental standards.

These requirements are designed to ensure the reliability, legal validity, and scientific integrity of breath alcohol testing in Utah law enforcement settings.[^1_2][^1_3][^1_4]

# according to this law if an intoxilyzer 8000 test was administered during a period of 144 seconds would that invalidate the test bc the blowing period was too long? if the test takes 144 seconds, should there be a retest

There is no specific language in Utah Admin Code R714-500-6 or associated publicly available Utah breath test procedures that sets an absolute maximum blowing period or total test duration (such as 144 seconds) for the Intoxilyzer 8000 that would automatically invalidate a test simply because it took that long. The rule instead focuses on instrument certification, pre-test deprivation periods, and test protocol standards to ensure scientific reliability.[^2_1][^2_2][^2_3]

What is clear from related legal sources and operator guides is that the standard test process‚Äîunder normal, properly functioning conditions‚Äîtakes much less than 144 seconds to complete the blowing and analysis segment for a single sample. Extended duration may indicate operator error, subject difficulty, or an instrument malfunction, but the rule does not state that an overlong test alone voids the result or strictly requires a retest.[^2_4]

However, for accuracy and legal reliability, if a breath sample takes far longer than normal (like 144 seconds), it could raise substantial concerns about proper administration, instrument accuracy, or procedural compliance, and may be grounds for challenging the result. In such scenarios, a retest is advised by best scientific and legal practice, though not strictly mandated by R714-500-6 language itself.[^2_1][^2_4]

A result obtained under these circumstances could potentially be suppressed or given less evidentiary weight in court, but exclusion is not automatic under the rule. Defense challenges would typically focus on procedural irregularities and their impact on the reliability of the measurement. For more definite exclusion, consult the full text of specific Utah Administrative Rules, machine manuals, or seek legal advice specialized in Utah DUI law.[^2_4][^2_1]

**I. INTRODUCTION**

**II. ARGUMENT**

#### A. **Utah Law Requires Strict Compliance With Protocols for Scientific Reliability**

**Utah Admin. Code R714-500-6** mandates that instruments used for breath alcohol testing be certified by the Department of Public Safety and ‚Äúprovide accurate and consistent analysis of breath specimen for the determination of breath alcohol concentration for law enforcement purposes.‚Äù The regulation further establishes that the test must be based on the principle of infra-red energy absorption and provide an ‚Äúessentially alveolar or end expiratory‚Äù sample of breath ([Cornell Law Utah Admin. Any instrument found ‚Äúunreliable or unserviceable‚Äù is subject to removal from service; Utah law therefore requires a retest or disqualification of results when a procedural or technical defect is manifest ([Utah Admin. Code R714-500-6](https://www.law.cornell.edu/regulations/utah/Utah-Admin-Code-R714-500-6)).

#### B. **Manufacturer Protocols Set Clear Duration and Sampling Requirements**

The official **Intoxilyzer 8000 Reference Guide** expressly requires that a valid sample be completed within the instrument‚Äôs scientifically calibrated time frame. The guide details that subjects generally have ‚Äúthree (3) minutes to provide a minimum acceptable breath sample‚Äù and that proper analysis ‚Äútakes several seconds‚Äù ([Intoxilyzer 8000 Reference Guide PDF](https://dui2go.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/I8000ReferenceGuide.pdf)). Supporting commentary from Utah legal authorities and DUI defense experts confirms that deviation from standardized protocol exposes the result to challenge for lack of scientific reliability ([Levitt Legal, Intoxilyzer 8000](https://defenselawutah.com/intoxilyzer-8000/); [Brown Bradshaw, Utah Breath Test](https://www.brownbradshaw.com/dui-law/breath-test)).

#### C. **Procedural Irregularity Renders the Result Unreliable and Inadmissible**

Here, the sole breath test at issue in the State‚Äôs case was conducted over an inexplicable duration‚Äî144 seconds‚Äîwithout reset, retest, or operator correction. This is well outside the anticipated protocols under both Utah law and manufacturer guidance and thus cannot form the reliable basis for criminal prosecution. ***

**III. CONCLUSION**

Because the Intoxilyzer 8000 test in question was administered in clear violation of statutory and scientific protocols‚Äîspecifically, completed over an excessive, abnormal duration without retest‚Äîthe result is invalid and must be suppressed. # State v. Mitchell, 31 Fla. L. Weekly Supp. 213a. (Polk Cty. Ct. 2023).

**State v. Mitchell, 31 Fla. L. Weekly Supp. 213a. (Polk Cty. Ct. 2023)** is a recent Florida DUI case in which the court granted a motion to suppress breath test results due to the police agency's failure to comply with the Implied Consent Law (Section 316.1932, Fla. Stat.) and Rule 11D-8 of the Florida Administrative Code governing the operation and inspection of breath test instruments.

The defense showed that agency inspections on the Intoxilyzer 8000 machine at the Lakeland Police Department repeatedly failed with error codes like ìambient fail,î ìcontrol outside tolerance,î and ìnon-compliance,î and that required inspection protocols (including documentation and corrective action) were not properly followed. The court found these failures amounted to a lack of "substantial compliance" with administrative rules, rendering the breathalyzer results inadmissible.

**Key findings and result:**

- Instrument failed multiple agency inspections and was nonetheless returned to evidentiary use without substantial compliance.
- Later inspections and repairs did not cure the noncompliance of the critical agency inspection.
- The court excluded the breath test from trial, leading the prosecutor to drop the only charge on the eve of trial.

This case reaffirms that in Florida, breath test results are only admissible if statutory and administrative procedures are strictly followed. Noncompliance in instrument inspection can result in suppression of breath evidence, which can be pivotal in defending DUI charges.[^1_1]

# State v. Swindle, 23 Fla. L. Weekly Supp. 613a (Hillsborough Cty Ct. 2015)

State v. Swindle, 23 Fla. L. Weekly Supp. 613a (Hillsborough Cty Ct. 2015), is a Florida County Court case involving DUI breath test evidence and compliance with procedural regulations for breathalyzer testing machines. The specific dispute centered on the approval and use of an alcohol reference solution (Lot \#201302B) in agency inspections of the Intoxilyzer 8000 device, which produced breath results used in the defendant's case.[^1_1]

### Core Holding

The court found that the Florida Department of Law Enforcement (FDLE) had improperly approved a lot of alcohol reference solution that was used in required monthly inspections. Notably, one result from initial testing of this lot was outside the acceptable range, which rendered the solution unapproved for use. Further, the court determined that retesting of the lot did not fulfill the requirements of the relevant administrative rule (FDLE Rule 11D-8.0035). The retesting was performed over two days and the court considered it a continuationónot a separate eventómeaning the failed original result could not be disregarded under the rules.[^1_1]

### Suppression of Evidence

Because the Intoxilyzer 8000 breathalyzer was inspected using a non-approved solution, the agencyís monthly inspections did not comply with statutory and regulatory requirements. As a result, the court granted the defendant's motion to suppress the breath test results. The prosecution was precluded from using implied consent law presumptions to admit the breath test evidence, as substantial compliance with Rule 11D-8.006 was absent.[^1_1]

### Impact and Related Cases

The Swindle decision is regularly referenced along with other Hillsborough County cases from the same period, where courts suppressed breath test evidence when procedural and regulatory mandatesóespecially relating to solution approval and machine inspectionówere not satisfied. These include State v. Liley and State v. Larkin, which further clarify the minimum standards for admissible breath test evidence in DUI cases.[^1_1]

### Key Legal Takeaway

Strict compliance with the FDLE regulations regarding breathalyzer maintenance and alcohol reference solution approval is required in order for the state to admit breath test results and use statutory presumptions in DUI prosecutions. Failure to do so will result in suppression of the breath test evidence.[^1_1]
