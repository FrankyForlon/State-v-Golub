1. FINAL PRETRIAL CONFERENCE (April 7, 2025 at 1:00 p.m.)

What Is Typically Covered
* Outstanding motions and evidentiary issues: The court often addresses any pending motions (e.g., motions to suppress, motions in limine to exclude certain evidence, etc.).
* Logistics and scheduling: The judge confirms that counsel are ready for trial, discusses how many days are needed, and verifies witness availability and any outstanding discovery.

The Purpose of the Final Pretrial Conference
* Ensure readiness: The court wants both sides to be fully prepared so trial proceeds smoothly.
* Resolve last-minute disputes: This conference is a final checkpoint for settling which evidence will come in or how certain issues will be handled, preventing trial delays.
* Confirm jury-trial logistics: The judge usually confirms the trial date, clarifies any last-minute scheduling issues, and ensures no reason exists for further postponement.

3. Submit/preview jury instructions (if required): Some courts request proposed jury instructions in advance, or at least a draft for discussion.
4. Coordinate with counsel and the court about trial length: Make sure the judge knows how long you anticipate for opening, witness testimony, and closing argument.
5. Raise any special requests: For instance, if you plan to present video evidence or need special equipment in court, address it now to ensure it’s set up for trial.
6. Confirm the plan for voir dire: If you have specific questions or need extra time for jury selection, mention it to the judge to see how the court will handle that process.

1. JURY SELECTION (VOIR DIRE)

GENERAL PRINCIPLES
• Selecting a fair-minded jury. In DUI trials, prospective jurors sometimes bring strong biases. Some believe DUI defendants are always guilty if stopped. Others are skeptical of any DUI evidence. An attorney’s goal is to uncover these leanings—both pro-state and pro-defense—to ensure the jury will follow the law and consider all evidence fairly.
• Addressing perceptions of law enforcement. Because one major angle in your case involves challenging the officer’s credibility, attorneys often examine whether prospective jurors (a) feel strongly inclined to trust police testimony, (b) have had bad experiences with law enforcement, or (c) feel strong pressure always to “back the blue.” Depending on the local court’s rules, attorneys might ask questions along these lines in voir dire to identify extreme biases.
• Highlighting the importance of presumption of innocence. Courts typically instruct jurors that the defendant is presumed innocent, and the state must prove each element beyond a reasonable doubt. Defense attorneys often emphasize this presumption during jury selection, checking whether potential jurors are truly willing to put aside personal beliefs about DUI or fear of drunk driving.
EXAMPLES OF GENERAL JURY SELECTION QUESTIONS
* “Is there anything about the nature of a DUI charge that makes you feel you could not be fair or impartial?”
* “Have you, your friends, or family had any experience with police, whether positive or negative, that might affect how you evaluate an officer’s testimony?”
* “Do you believe a police officer’s testimony should be automatically trusted, or do you believe it should be evaluated the same way you evaluate any other witness?”

2. TRIAL ARGUMENTS FOR THE DEFENSE

GENERAL PRINCIPLES
• Challenges to the Stop (Fourth Amendment). Defense attorneys frequently argue that the initial traffic stop was unlawful if the officer did not have a valid reason. Many of the documents you mention (motions to suppress, transcripts referencing vantage points, dashcam video, etc.) can be used to cast doubt on whether the officer properly observed a moving violation.
* Common angle: Argue the officer lacked a line of sight or probable cause. For example, if camera footage shows the officer was too far back or otherwise obstructed from seeing the defendant’s car fail to signal, attorneys may argue the entire stop was invalid and all subsequent evidence (field sobriety tests, breath tests) must be suppressed.
• Procedural/constitutional violations during detention or questioning (Fifth Amendment). Attorneys frequently point out any failure to provide Miranda warnings before custodial interrogation. If a court agrees that statements were taken in violation of the defendant’s rights, those statements can be suppressed.
* Common angle: If the defense can show the officer delayed Miranda warnings until the defendant effectively incriminated himself, or manipulated him into speaking, the defense can argue to exclude those statements.
• Accuracy of Field Sobriety Tests (FSTs). Even if the officer claims the defendant “failed” certain tasks, attorneys often highlight procedural missteps: incomplete instructions, questionable testing conditions (weather, terrain, time of night), questionable health or medical conditions, or an officer’s subjective scoring.
* Common angle: If transcripts reflect that the officer found “6/6 clues” for Horizontal Gaze Nystagmus but gave contradictory testimony on how the test was administered, or if the video shows the defendant standing steadily, an attorney might use the inconsistency to argue the officer exaggerated.
• Reliability of Breath Tests (Intoxilyzer). Where machine reliability is in doubt, attorneys often present (1) known error rates, (2) calibration records, (3) the required breath-sample protocols, or (4) prior rulings excluding a certain device’s results.
* Common angle: Show that the Intoxilyzer 8000 is subject to recognized malfunctions—especially if only one breath sample was taken, or if the machine required an unusually long sample. Raise the specter that incorrectly calibrated machines or operator error can produce inflated results.

3. PROSECUTION ARGUMENTS AND OBJECTIONS (AND WAYS TO COUNTER)

GENERAL PRINCIPLES
• Prosecution’s likely narrative:
1. There was a legitimate stop (the defendant broke a traffic law).
2. The officer observed signs of impairment—odor of alcohol, bloodshot eyes, slurred speech, poor balance, etc.
3. Field sobriety tests indicated impairment.
4. A breath or blood test result was above the legal limit, or else there was “appreciable impairment” under your local DUI standard.
• Likely objections from the state:
* Hearsay: If the defense tries to introduce out-of-court statements or question the officer on statements not in evidence.
* Relevance: If the defense tries to dive deep into officer’s past or unrelated cases without establishing legal relevance or a good-faith basis.
* Speculation: If the defense asks the officer to guess about the defendant’s state of mind or to interpret evidence the officer didn’t personally observe.
• Ways defense often counters:
* Foundation: Show the officer’s prior statements are relevant to credibility. If the officer gave contradictory testimony in a hearing or wrote inconsistent details in the police report, the defense can typically question him on those specifics.
* Bias or motive: Argue that the officer’s personal style, “stop-to-arrest ratio,” or desire to secure convictions is a form of bias. If there’s a basis to suggest systemic issues (e.g., the officer frequently leaves details out or has previously been found to misrepresent facts), it can be fair to ask about this—so long as it’s within the court’s evidentiary constraints.
* Impeachment: Under normal rules of evidence, prior inconsistent statements are valid impeachment material. If transcripts show the officer testified one way in a prior hearing and changed details later, the defense can highlight that shift to attack credibility.

4. CONNECTING WITH THE JURY

GENERAL PRINCIPLES
• Theme and story. A defense theme might be that the officer “jumped to conclusions” or “saw what he expected to see,” rather than what actually happened. In a DUI case, attorneys sometimes illustrate everyday experiences: “Everyone occasionally wobbles or loses balance, especially at night, on uneven pavement, or when anxious.” This helps jurors see that the same movements an officer calls “signs of impairment” could have innocent explanations—stress, fatigue, or nerves.
• Underscoring reasonableness. Jurors often want to see if the defendant’s conduct could just as easily be consistent with sobriety as with impairment. Point to portions of the video where the defendant seems steady, or the officer’s contradictory statements about how many times the defendant stepped off the line.
• Be respectful, not accusatory. Even when attacking the officer’s credibility, defense attorneys often aim to do so in a calm, factual way that resonates with everyday experience. Jurors often dislike a purely personal attack on law enforcement. Summarizing contradictory statements factually and letting the jury conclude “something’s off here” can be more powerful than direct accusations of lying.
• Invoking fairness and burden of proof. A staple defense argument is reminding jurors the burden is entirely on the state. The jury doesn’t have to prove the officer is corrupt or incompetent—only that the evidence leaves reasonable doubt about whether the defendant was legally impaired.

5. SHIFTING FOCUS TO THE ARRESTING OFFICER’S CREDIBILITY

GENERAL PRINCIPLES
• Impeachment by Contradiction. If an officer’s police report and hearing testimony differ from video evidence—or from the second officer’s statements—defense counsel can use each discrepancy to erode credibility. Even “small” lies or omissions can matter in a DUI trial; if the officer seems cavalier about details, the jury may wonder whether the rest of the account is inflated.
• Pattern or practice. If you have legitimate, court-admissible evidence that the officer has a track record of questionable arrests, attorneys may try to introduce that, subject to the rules of evidence (which typically require a showing of relevance and a good-faith basis). Courts can limit how much prior history is introduced, so attorneys usually must show why it directly bears on this arrest’s reliability.
• Cross-examination approach. It is common for a defense attorney to proceed step by step through the officer’s claims, using transcript quotes or video time stamps:
* “At the last hearing, you claimed you were alone for the entire search, correct? Now let’s look at the video, showing a second officer. Please explain.”
* “You testified that you read Miranda warnings before asking about drinks, correct? But in your motion-suppress hearing, you said otherwise. Now which version is correct?”
* “You wrote in your affidavit that the defendant was unsteady on the one-leg stand, but your field-sobriety scoring marked 0 out of 4 clues. Please clarify.”
• Highlight the officer’s vantage point or incomplete memory. If the testimony contradicts the dashcam or bodycam position (for instance, the camera’s angle shows no line of sight, or the officer claims to see a turn signal that the camera reveals was actually active), it can directly undermine the officer’s credibility.
• Avoid overreach. A common pitfall is going so hard after the officer that jurors become sympathetic to law enforcement. Effective counsel often focuses on presenting objective contradictions, letting the jury come away thinking, “If these are the problems we can see, what else is being glossed over?”

PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER: A POSSIBLE “BROAD-STROKES” TRIAL OUTLINE
Below is a general outline some attorneys might follow to organize all elements of a DUI defense. Again, this is not specific legal advice; consult a licensed attorney for a defense tailored to any actual case.
1. Pretrial Motions (e.g., Motions to Suppress, Dismiss).
o Raise constitutional arguments: invalid traffic stop, no probable cause.
o Challenge breath or blood test reliability if local rules allow an evidentiary hearing.
2. Jury Selection (Voir Dire).
o Identify strong biases regarding DUIs, police testimony.
o Emphasize presumption of innocence and the state’s burden.

o Keep it simple: the officer’s story does not match the video, or evidence does not prove impairment beyond a reasonable doubt.
o Promise the jurors they will see how the state’s evidence has errors and contradictions.
4. Cross-Examination of the Officer(s).
o Use transcripts, video time stamps, police reports to spotlight any discrepancies.
o Gently yet persistently explore contradictions about the defendant’s supposed “impairment” vs. the officer’s own field-sobriety scoring or the video’s actual depiction.

o If relevant, highlight potential calibration issues, operator errors, or known reliability concerns about the Intoxilyzer 8000 (especially if the local court has recognized them).

o If the defendant testifies (a carefully weighed decision in DUI cases), counsel typically prepares them thoroughly to explain any confusion and highlight consistent, sober actions.

o Walk jurors through each step. Show the officer’s story is unsupported or at least raises doubt.

o Remind the jury that the defense need not prove innocence; the state must remove all reasonable doubt.

IMPORTANT REMINDERS
• Local Rules Vary. DUI procedures and legal standards can differ widely among jurisdictions—even within the same state. • Motions and Objections Are Complex. They often involve detailed procedural rules. If your jurisdiction requires motion notices or particular forms, be mindful of deadlines and required written support.
• Avoid Personal Attacks. While pointing out inconsistencies, defense counsel typically remains respectful to keep the jury’s confidence. Credibility challenges work best when grounded in specific facts (transcript lines, video footage time stamps, the officer’s own contradictory references).
• Consult a DUI Defense Attorney. Because DUIs can lead to serious consequences (loss of license, fines, jail time, a criminal record), an attorney familiar with local DUI law can better tailor arguments to local precedents and ensure the defense is raised effectively and lawfully.

Supporting Evidence:

* Officer's Repeated Commands: Cpl. Ernstsen instructed Mr. Golub to "keep going" at least 18 times during the test, pushing him beyond normal testing parameters.
* Excessive Volume Collection: The test recorded a breath volume of 2.039 liters, exceeding the standard requirement of 1.1 to 1.5 liters by approximately 33%.
* Absence of Secondary Verification: Despite the anomalous testing conditions, no secondary test was conducted to verify the results.
* Officer's Limited Understanding: When questioned about the Intoxilyzer operation (Hearing II, [00:36:08]), Cpl. Ernstsen described it as "pretty simple. We just push this little green button that says start," demonstrating limited understanding of the device's technical operation.
4. Fatigue as Alternative Explanation
Argument: Mr. Golub's condition was the result of extreme fatigue from a 13-hour workday rather than alcohol impairment.
Supporting Evidence:
* Mr. Golub's Statement (Video IV, [00:20:15]): "I've been teaching since 7:30 [later clarified as 7:15] till 8 p.m. I teach in the Granite School District and then I teach at the University of Utah."
* Professional Context: As an educator with multiple teaching positions, Mr. Golub had completed a demanding workday before the stop.
* Sleep Medication: Mr. Golub mentioned having taken a sleep aid (doxylamine succinate), which can cause drowsiness and disorientation.
* Consistent with Observed Symptoms: Bloodshot eyes, slower response time, and mild coordination issues are all consistent with extreme fatigue.
* Successful Balance Performance: Despite fatigue, Mr. Golub performed perfectly on the One-Leg Stand test, suggesting his fundamental coordination remained intact.
5. Officer Credibility Issues
Argument: Cpl. Ernstsen's investigation and testimony demonstrate bias, inconsistency, and procedural lapses that undermine his credibility.
Supporting Evidence:
* Omission of Second Officer: Despite video evidence showing a second officer's substantial involvement, Cpl. Ernstsen failed to document this officer's presence and participation.
* Contradictory Testimony on Arrest Procedures: Cpl. Ernstsen claimed he personally placed Mr. Golub under arrest, but video evidence shows both officers participated in the handcuffing process.

* Targeted Enforcement: Dashcam footage shows Cpl. Ernstsen cruising at reduced speeds near establishments serving alcohol, suggesting targeted enforcement rather than response to observed violations.
* Phone Access Attempt: After reading Miranda rights, Cpl. Ernstsen attempted to access Mr. Golub's phone and listen to a private conversation with his mother, constituting an attempt at unwarranted surveillance.

Prosecution Counterarguments and Defense Responses
1. "Officer Ernstsen is an experienced DUI specialist with proper training."
Response: Experience does not excuse procedural violations. Cpl. Ernstsen's excessive stop-to-arrest ratio (23.29%) compared to state and national averages (1-6%) suggests bias rather than expertise. His demonstrated lack of understanding of the Intoxilyzer's operation ("just push this little green button") undermines claims of specialized knowledge.
2. "The 0.104 BAC result proves impairment regardless of test administration."
Response: The validity of the result depends entirely on proper test administration. When a test is conducted improperly—with excessive duration and volume—the result becomes scientifically unreliable. Multiple jurisdictions have excluded Intoxilyzer 8000 results due to reliability concerns, including Massachusetts in Commonwealth v. Camblin (2017).
3. "Field sobriety tests showed multiple clues of impairment."
Response: The tests were improperly administered and selectively interpreted. The Walk-and-Turn test was demonstrated incorrectly, and the One-Leg Stand test—the most objective balance assessment—showed zero clues of impairment. This selective documentation pattern suggests confirmation bias rather than objective assessment.
4. "Mr. Golub admitted to drinking and believed he would 'blow over.'"
Response: Mr. Golub admitted to having two beers over an hour before driving, an amount unlikely to cause significant impairment. His statement about "blowing over" was made under duress, without explanation of what "over" meant, and could reflect confusion about Utah's relatively low 0.05 legal limit rather than an admission of substantial impairment.
5. "Procedural imperfections don't negate the evidence of impairment."
Response: When procedural violations are this extensive and systematic—from the questionable initial stop to the improper FST administration to the flawed chemical testing—they undermine the integrity of the entire investigation. These aren't minor imperfections but fundamental failures that call into question all evidence obtained.
